In Arabic grammar, the default <dfn title="vowel-sound: aa, ee, oo, aan, een, and oon">harakaat</dfn> that goes on all words is dumma (ُ or ٌ, the &#8220;oo&#8221; or &#8220;oon&#8221; sound). When you don&#8217;t know the vowel, apply dumma. Other harakaat are for other cases&#8211;for example, posessive case takes kasra (ِ or ٍ, the &#8220;ee&#8221; or &#8220;een&#8221; sound).

Harakaat are two types&#8211;short (aa, ee, and oo) and long (aan, een, and oon).

The second point to remember is definitivity. A definitive object means a specific object, not just any old object. For example, the word kitaabun (كِتَابٌ) means &#8220;a book&#8221;, while al-kitaabu (الكِتَابُ) means &#8220;the book&#8221;. Al-kitaabu is definite, kitaabun is not definite.

The rule is that <span class="gem">definitive words don&#8217;t take tanween. They always take the single harakaat.</span> So if you want to turn &#8220;a camel&#8221; (جَمَلٌ) &#8220;jamaloon&#8221; into &#8220;the camel&#8221;, it becomes al-jamalu (الجَمَلُ). The same applies to all nouns (and to adjectives).
